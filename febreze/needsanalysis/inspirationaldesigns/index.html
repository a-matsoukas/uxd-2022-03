Inspirational Designs

Google Maps - Full team

  • Good for browsing — “What to see”, “highlights”, etc at top, as well as the “local favorites”
    • Local favorites is a filter to see what the people who live there really like, ensuring you are not falling in any “tourist traps” or similar
  • Map and thumbnail/list view side-by-side, good for filtering and seeing locations of things in list
    • Probably wouldn’t work (as a side-by-side scenario) on a phone — would need separate pages like Airbnb
  • Hotels and stuff accessible from that same home page
  • Bookmarking/saving for later
    • Also good for browsing

irl App - Anna

  • Very social
    • If your friends/community use it you are more likely to use it
  • Has you join groups when you sign up so you have things to look at right away
  • Easy to make connections with others through chats that are open to anyone
    • Sense of community
  • Easy to digest information about an event
    • Date, description, etc. Is in the same place for each event
    • Very clear and easy to send to a friend

Airbnb - Jacob

  • Accessibility filters (From Airbnb’s accessibility page)
    • In this case it’s mostly for wheelchair users — ideally our project will support other mobility impairments specifically, but the categories of features is a nice way to split this up. I like the two-column view too, it lets you see a lot more at once.
  • Search/browse inside map area or see list
    • Searching/browsing has two modes — you can look at a specific area of the map and it will display locations with their prices, or you can use the list view, which shows images and other info too. I do wish that you could filter the list view to a specific area using the map view, but this might be a little bit confusing (I think it might actually do this but it’s hard to tell, hence confusing).
  • After a stay, it asks if certain features of the place are still there/working
    • This time-sensitive property is one of the main ideas of our project’s usefulness. Airbnb asks a series of questions, like “does the place still have a dedicated workspace?”, but I think in our case you would manually select specific accessibility features and report if they are there/functioning or not.
  • Lots of fun categorical filters for the type of place or the general locale (seen at the top of the search screenshots)
    • This is just kind of fun for browsing, and something like it but less climate/environment-based (so you can stay focused in one general area) could be good for our project.

Lonely Planet - Carlo

  • List of top attractions in any place: Despite this being an options on many websites or apps, what I think sets Lonely Planet apart is that it is ALWAYS right. After a visit to the website, you can be sure that you know about all the hidden gems the place has to offer, not missing anything.
  • Good aesthetics and design: Unless your idea is completely revolutionary, I feel that what makes or breaks the success of a product is the aesthetics of it. Does it look modern and up to date? Is it easy, intuitive, and comfortable to use?
  • (Almost) custom made itineraries: whenever you open a page, you can select articles like “the ultimate itinerary for a weekend in boston” if you only are planning for two days, or “Milan’s must-see museums: a design for life” if you are particularly interested in visiting museums.

Netflix

  • Suggested for you: Netflix uses the shows you have already seen to calculate how much you will like a show, and provides a “compatibility rating”.

Trivago - Sree

  • Location filtering options
    • I like how Trivago lists the popular sites/locations when you enter a city, so implementing something similar could give users options into look at places that are currently trending/popular if they are having a hard time figuring out where to go.
  • Additional filtering options (including wheelchair accessibility)
    • Adding a section with “popular filters” would be convenient, as well as having the option to choose between places with different “stars” that other users can choose to rate a place with.
  • Full list of amenities
    • We could potentially use a similar format to list all possible accessible amenities for users to select/deselect

Waze - Max

  • Real time road updates
    • Waze Provides real time updates to its users as seen in the first two (left to right) photos above. When calculating and traveling along a route, Waze will adapt to user inputted and satellite data regarding, traffic, road obstructions, speed traps, etc. I found this feature of Waze to be inspirational because I feel that these constant updates would be crucial in our user interface. Traveling in a city as a person with a disability that effects mobility is already hard enough, but it begins to get even more difficult when a ramp you expected to travel on is under construction or broken, or a surface is being cleaned and is extremely difficult to get across on crutches without slipping. Having real time updates in our UI could mitigate these issues.
  • User reported data
    • I was also inspired by the collection and use of user data in Waze. The top rightmost photo shows how users can report real time road updates to be used on the map. This contributes to what I was describing above. This would be applicable to our interface because people could report accessibility constraints at places, so that others with mobility constraints can know whether or not they can travel to or participate in an activity in that area.
  • Users can edit and update map
    • I found this feature of Waze to be quite inspirational. It allows users to update the map that the Waze app uses to navigate drivers so that the map is constantly accurate and not solely based on one stream of data (satellite). Also, users of this service typically work together over messaging apps and group meetings based on location to ensure accuracy of updates. Some form of this could be included in our interface where users could edit the accessibility features of places in each city, and there could be groups of natives from each city that could communicate with each other and visitors to improve awareness of accessibility features and changes.
